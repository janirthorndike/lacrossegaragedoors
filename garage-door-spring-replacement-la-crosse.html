Broken Garage Door Spring in La Crosse?

La Crosse Garage Doors replaces broken torsion and extension springs for homes across La Crosse, Onalaska, Holmen, West Salem, and La Crescent — with a firm quote upfront and no after-hours surcharge. If your car's trapped behind a door that won't lift, this is the repair we built the company around.

A broken spring is the most common garage door failure there is — and the most misunderstood. The springs, not the opener, do the real work of lifting the door. When one snaps, you'll usually hear a loud bang like a firecracker, and the next morning the door won't budge or the opener strains and gives up. That's not an opener problem. That's a spring.

Torsion vs. extension springs

Most doors use one of two systems. Torsion springs mount on a metal shaft above the door and wind up to lift it — they're what you'll find on most modern doors, especially double-car doors. Extension springs run along the horizontal tracks on either side and stretch as the door closes, more common on older or single-car doors. We replace both, and we'll tell you which you have when we look.

Why we replace both springs

If your door has two springs and only one broke, we'll recommend replacing the pair. They're installed together, they wear at the same rate, and a spring is rated for roughly 10,000 open-close cycles. When one reaches the end of its life, the other is almost always right behind it. Replacing both at once means you're not paying for a second service call a few months from now — and it keeps the door balanced, which is easier on your opener.

Don't keep hitting the opener. Running the opener against a broken spring can burn out the motor, snap a cable, or send the door crashing down. If the door won't lift, leave it — and if your car is stuck inside, we can almost always release and raise it by hand when we arrive.

What the repair looks like

Spring replacement is a same-day job for us in the vast majority of cases. We confirm the system and door weight, install the correct replacement springs, re-balance the door so it holds at any height, check the cables and rollers while we're in there, and cycle it several times to make sure everything runs smooth and quiet. You get a firm quote before we start — nothing gets done until you've okayed the number.

Common questions

Spring replacement, answered

How much does spring replacement cost?

It depends on a few things: whether you have a torsion or extension system, single or double door, and whether one or both springs need replacing. What doesn't change is how we quote it — a firm number upfront, before any work starts, with no surprise fees and no after-hours surcharge.

Want a ballpark before we head out? Just ask when you reach out and we'll give you an honest range for your setup.

Can I open the door with a broken spring?

You shouldn't. The springs carry the door's full weight — often 150 to 300 pounds — so without them the opener strains, cables can snap, and the door can fall. If your car's trapped inside, leave the door alone; we can usually raise it by hand on arrival.

If only one spring broke, why replace both?

On a two-spring door, both springs are the same age and wear at the same rate. When one goes, the other is usually close behind. Replacing both at once saves you a second service call within months and keeps the door balanced.

Why do springs always break in the cold?

Steel gets brittle in freezing temperatures, so a spring near the end of its life tends to give out on the coldest mornings. It's the most common winter door failure around La Crosse — more on that on our cold-weather page.
