Winter troubleshooting

Garage Door Won't Open in the Cold?

In a La Crosse winter, a door that worked fine in October suddenly won't budge in January. Here's what's usually going on, what you can safely check yourself, and when it's time to call.

Cold weather is hard on garage doors, and the La Crosse area gets plenty of it. When a door quits on a freezing morning, it's almost always one of four things. Work through them in order — the first two you can often handle yourself.

1. The door is frozen to the slab

The rubber weather seal along the bottom of the door can freeze to the concrete overnight, especially after snow-melt or rain followed by a hard freeze. The opener strains against a door that's essentially glued down.

What to do: Don't hold the button down trying to force it — that strains the motor and can tear the seal. Pour warm (not boiling) water along the bottom edge, or run a hair dryer on the seam, then gently break the door loose and lift it by hand before using the opener again.

2. The remote or keypad battery is dead

Cold drains small batteries fast. If the door doesn't respond to the remote or keypad but the wall button inside works fine, that's your answer — swap the battery. It's the simplest fix on this list and worth ruling out first.

3. A spring has broken

This is the big one. Steel gets brittle in the cold, so a spring near the end of its life is most likely to snap on the coldest morning of the year — a broken spring is the most common winter door failure we see. If you heard a loud bang (often the night before), and now the door won't lift or the opener hums and gives up, suspect the spring.

Stop using the opener. Running it against a broken spring can burn out the motor or drop the door. This one isn't a DIY fix — springs are under extreme tension. See our spring replacement page, and if your car's trapped inside, we can usually raise the door by hand on arrival.

4. Stiff lubricant or opener force settings

Old grease on the tracks and rollers thickens in the cold and adds resistance. Combined with a door that's contracted and stiff, that can be enough to stop an opener whose force setting is set low — it'll start, struggle, and reverse. A proper fix is fresh garage-door-rated lubricant on the rollers, hinges, and springs, and, if needed, a technician adjusting the opener's force setting safely. (Force settings are a safety feature — best not to crank them up yourself without knowing why the door got harder to lift.)

A quick winter checklist

Before you call, run through these: try the wall button instead of the remote; check whether the bottom seal is iced to the concrete; pull the red emergency release cord and try lifting the door by hand. If it lifts smoothly by hand, the problem is the opener or tracks. If it's extremely heavy or won't move — and especially if you heard a bang — you likely have a broken spring, and you should stop and call rather than keep trying.

Cold-weather FAQ

Why won't my door open when it's freezing?

Usually one of four things: the bottom seal is frozen to the concrete, a spring has broken, the lubricant has thickened, or the opener's force setting is too low for the added resistance. Cold also kills remote batteries. Check them in that order.

It's frozen to the ground — how do I free it?

Don't force it with the opener. Pour warm water along the bottom edge or use a hair dryer on the seam, break it loose gently, and lift by hand before using the opener.

Do springs really break more in winter?

Yes — brittle cold steel plus a spring near the end of its life means the coldest mornings are when they snap. It's the most common winter failure we see. If the door won't lift and you heard a bang, suspect the spring and stop using the opener.
